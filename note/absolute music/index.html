absolute music

During the [[Romanticism]], the concept of absolute music emerged. It's the idea that music can be stand-alone, and doesn't need external narrative or concept to be valuable.

Wagner coined the term for his own performance of Beethoven's Ninth Symphony. Where he mentions that in the final movement, the piece leaves behind its pure instrumental (absolute) character and introduces voice and text.

During these times, music was valuable when it was able to achieve a certain purity. And it fits the art for art's sake ethos of the period. Music didn't need a practical, moral, or didactic purpose; it can be valued for its own beauty and intrinsic qualities.

[[Ernst Theodor Wilhelm Hoffmann|Hoffman]] calls absolute music (even thought he doesn't use the term) the most romantic of all arts–because it's not mixed with any other arts.

[[Georg Wilhelm Friedrich Hegel|Hegel]], a contemporary of Beethoven described a 'self-sufficient music' by getting rid of the alien element of language. And, the value of the piece increasing by having no poetry in the music.

[[Carl Dahlhaus|Dahlhaus]] describes absolute music as:

The idea of ‘absolute music’ [..] consists of the conviction that instrumental music purely and clearly expresses ==the true nature of music by its very lack of concept, object, and purpose==. Not its existence, but what it stands for, is decisive. Instrumental music, as ‘pure structure,’ represents itself. (Dahlhaus 1989a, 7)
