aesthetics in music

Aeshetics is concerned with the perception and how we value music as an art form.

Aesthetic judgment refers to the process of evaluating or appreciating something based on its beauty or artistic value, rather than its practical utility or factual content.

[[Baumgarten]] in his Meditationes philosphicae de nonnullis ad poema pertinentibus (1735), makes the distinction between knowledge and perception and their respective faculaties, superior and lower. Things peceived in the science of perception are then in the lower faculty: aesthetic.

So, aeshetics can be understood in relation to [[interpretation]] ([[hermeneutics]]).

Later, and as a critique, [[Kant]]–in his Kritik der Urteilskraft (1790)–concerns himself with how an aesthetic quality can be both perceived and rationalised.

To make an aesthetic judgement (that is both perceived and rational), Kant says we'd need to see beauty from four perspectives:

  • Quality: Kant advocates for disinterested, detached aesthetic judgment, seeking true appreciation of art beyond personal biases, yet risking disconnection from the artwork's real-world context. ([[absolute music|absolute]], [[autonomy]], [[formalism]])
  • Quantity: If we perceive the art work to be beautiful, others should be able to experience the same qualities. This shifts the act of judgement away from subjectivity and is different from Baumgarten's lower faculty.
  • Purpose: Kant believes aesthetic judgments are based on a work's inherent patterns, structures, and 'formal finality', focusing on how it forms a complete, unified entity. (Not related external concepts.)
  • Exemplary: When someone makes an aesthetic judgment about a piece of art, this judgment might become a kind of model or benchmark for how others might view or value that art. ([[Music can bring certain order and help interiorize certain values like maintaining social harmony#^582c3a|social/historical disciplining]])

Kant views music's transience (inability to articulate recognisable concepts) as a weakness, as it cannot articulate precise [[meaning]].

[[Hegel]], disagrees with Kant, and views the transience it as a tool for self-realisation. He views artworks as embodying instrinsic beauty and significance. Contributing the humanity's deepest interests.

The 19th century saw increased philosophical engagement with music, including [[Nietzsche]] and [[Schopenhauer]], emphasizing heightened subjectivity and meaning in music.

Schopenhauer, regards music as highly significant and uniquely representing his concept of the Will. He sees it as an art form that deeply resonates with humans and transcends the clarity of the phenomenal world.

In the 20th century, the significance of music and its aesthetic understanding evolved under the influence of [[modernism]] and [[critical theory]], leading to new perspectives and critiques.

Lawrence [[Kramer]]'s work Musical Meaning: Toward a Critical History revisits the question of musical meaning, bringing a contemporary dimension to the debate and encompassing a diverse range of music, challenging traditional aesthetic interpretations.

[!warning]
The question remains whether common interpretative strategies can be applied across different musical contexts, or if different music types demand unique aesthetic responses. Like, can beauty be found in metal? What about the social disciplining of Sleep Token?
